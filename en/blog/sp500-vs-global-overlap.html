S&P 500 and a Global Index Together: How Much Overlap, and Is It Bad?

9 min read · By the WorthOne team

It may be the most common portfolio among beginner investors: a bit of S&P 500, and a bit of a "global index" (like MSCI ACWI or MSCI World). The logic sounds perfect — exposure to strong America, plus broad global diversification. But when you put these two funds into an overlap calculator, many are surprised to find they overlap by about 60%. How is that possible? And what does it mean for the portfolio? Let's make sense of it.

Why is there overlap at all?

The secret lies in how a global index is built. A global index doesn't split money evenly between countries — it weights them by market value. And because American companies are the largest in the world, the US alone makes up about 63% of the global ACWI index. In other words, when you buy a "global index," you're already buying mostly America.

And it runs deeper: the largest companies in the S&P 500 — Apple, Microsoft, Nvidia, Amazon, Google — are the very same companies sitting at the top of the global index. So when you hold both the S&P 500 and a global index, you're buying those same giants twice. That's the overlap: not a coincidence, but built into the structure.

The numerical example

Suppose a 50/50 portfolio: 50% S&P 500 and 50% global ACWI. The S&P is all US, and ACWI is 63% US. Simple arithmetic: 50% + (50% × 63%) = about 81.5% exposure to the US. A portfolio that looks "half American, half global" is in practice almost entirely American, with a sprinkle of the rest of the world. This is exactly the insight the overlap calculator surfaces — and it surprises most people who enter it for the first time.

So is it bad? Not necessarily

Here it's important to pause and be precise: holding these two funds together is not a "mistake." It's simply a tilt toward the US — a decision to give the American market a larger weight than it gets in the standard global index. An investor holding 50/50 is saying, consciously or not, "I want more America than 63%." That's a perfectly legitimate stance. Many investors hold it deliberately.

The problem isn't the tilt itself — it's the lack of awareness of it. The real danger is an investor who thinks "I have a global fund, so I'm nicely diversified," while in practice 81% of their money depends on a single market. This is the "illusion of diversification": the feeling that you're protected while you're actually making a large, concentrated bet. Awareness is the difference between an informed decision and an unpleasant surprise.

The advantages: why people choose a US tilt

  • Past performance. Over the past decade or two, the US market has delivered impressive returns that led the world. It must be said immediately: past returns do not guarantee future returns — but this is what draws many.
  • The center of innovation. The companies leading the technology, semiconductor and AI revolutions are mostly American. Those who believe this trend will continue want heightened exposure to it.
  • Simplicity and familiarity. The S&P 500 is the world's best-known index, and it's easier to connect with emotionally than a list of thousands of companies from countries you've never heard of.

The real meaning: what the risk is

Beyond the illusion of diversification, a large US tilt carries two concrete meanings:

  • Country concentration. If the US market enters a weak decade — which has happened before — the bulk of the portfolio falls with it, without a "cushion" from other regions that might actually rise during that period.
  • Sector concentration. The US index is heavily tilted to technology. A US tilt is, indirectly, also a tech-sector tilt — another layer of concentration many don't notice.

The strong argument: why most of the weight might belong on the global index

The million-dollar question: if you hold both, is it better for most of the weight to be on the global index? There's a strong, widely-held argument that says "yes," and it rests on one principle: you don't know who will win the next decade, and you don't have to.

History teaches humility. In the late 1980s, Japan was the superstar of stock markets — at its peak it made up about 40% of global market value, and investors were sure it was the future. Then came the "lost decades": a Japanese market that stagnated for decades. Whoever bet everything on yesterday's winner paid dearly.

Here lies the beauty of a global index: it corrects itself automatically. When a country flourishes, its market value grows and its weight in the index rises on its own. When it weakens, the weight falls. You never need to guess the next star, sell at the right time or buy at the right time — the index "rides" the winners of every era without you doing a thing. It's a diversification machine that updates itself.

And a point that surprises many: if most of your weight is on the global index, you're still heavily exposed to the US — because it's already 63% of it. So "tilting the weight to global" doesn't mean giving up on America; it just means not doubling it beyond its natural weight. You get all the American giants, plus a global safety cushion — without picking a side.

This is why the most "neutral" approach in passive investing is simply to hold one broad global index, with no tilts. Someone who still adds the S&P 500 does so to amplify the US — and that's an active choice, legitimate as long as it's a conscious one.

The other side: when a US tilt makes sense

In fairness — some would argue a US tilt is justified: the American market is the most transparent, liquid and regulated in the world; it contains most of the global companies that already operate everywhere; and its legal and economic system is stable. The point isn't that a US tilt is "forbidden" — it's that it should be a decision, not an accident.

The bottom line

Holding both the S&P 500 and a global index isn't a mistake, but it's also not necessarily what it seems. The two overlap by about 60%, and the result is a strong tilt to the US. The right question isn't "am I allowed" but "am I aware." If you want a heightened US weight and choose it deliberately — great. If you thought you were globally diversified while you're almost entirely in America — it's worth knowing.
