Study Fund vs. Provident Fund for Investment

6 min read · By the WorthOne team

These two vehicles appear with almost every Israeli saver, and are often confused. Both let you save and invest in the capital market, but they differ fundamentally in their tax benefits and liquidity rules.

The study fund (keren hishtalmut)

The study fund is one of Israel's most attractive savings vehicles, mainly thanks to an exemption from capital gains tax (up to a recognized annual deposit ceiling). It is usually liquid after six years, and for employees part of the deposit is funded by the employer. It's an excellent tool for medium-term saving.

The provident fund for investment

A provident fund for investment is liquid at any time – you can withdraw the money whenever you want (paying regular capital gains tax on the profit). Its unique advantage: if you leave the balance until retirement age and withdraw it as an annuity, you may enjoy a tax exemption. There is an annual deposit ceiling per saver.

The key differences

  • Liquidity: the provident fund is always liquid; the study fund usually after six years.
  • Tax: the study fund offers an exemption on profit up to the ceiling; in the provident fund the exemption depends on withdrawing as an annuity at retirement.
  • Employer contribution: exists in the study fund for employees, not in the provident fund.

How to choose

There's no single answer for everyone. If you have access to a study fund (especially with employer matching) – it's usually an excellent starting point. A provident fund for investment fits when full flexibility matters, or once you've maxed out the study fund ceiling. Either way, review the management fees and the investment track.
