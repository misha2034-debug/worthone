Liquid Net Worth: How Much of Your Money Is Actually Available?

7 min read · By the WorthOne team

In Israel the term "הון עצמי" (equity / net worth) carries two meanings that are easy to confuse. The first is your total net worth — everything you own minus everything you owe. The second comes from real estate: the equity you bring to buy a home, the money you put in yourself when taking a mortgage. What links the two is a single idea: how much money is really available to you, here and now. And that is exactly what "liquidity" measures.

A quick reminder: what net worth is

Total net worth is the sum of your assets minus the sum of your liabilities. If you have ₪400,000 in assets and ₪100,000 in loans, your net worth is ₪300,000. It's the basic measure of financial health, and it's exactly what our calculator shows. But there's a subtle point most people miss: not every shekel of net worth is worth the same in terms of availability.

Liquid, semi-liquid, and locked

Liquidity is how quickly you can turn an asset into cash without paying a price for it. Here's the spectrum, from most available to most locked:

  • Fully liquid: checking account, accessible deposits, money-market funds. The money is there within a day or two, with no penalty.
  • Liquid within a short time: an investment portfolio and ETFs in a trading account. You can sell and withdraw within a few days — but timing depends on the market, and capital-gains tax may apply.
  • Semi-liquid: a study fund after six years, a provident fund for investment. You can reach the money, but early withdrawal may cost you a tax benefit or a penalty.
  • Locked: a pension fund (blocked until retirement), a study fund before six years, and also real estate — an apartment is a valuable asset, but selling it takes months, and you can't "withdraw" ₪20,000 from it for an emergency.

Liquid net worth is, broadly, the sum of the assets in the first two categories: the money you could put on the table within a few days without breaking anything.

Why this distinction matters so much

Picture two people with exactly the same net worth — ₪500,000. The first owns an apartment worth ₪450,000 plus ₪50,000 in liquid savings. The second holds ₪500,000 in a money-market fund and an investment portfolio. On paper they're identical, but if the car breaks down tomorrow or they lose their job — the second can handle it easily, while the first may be forced into expensive debt, despite being "worth half a million."

That's why an emergency fund is always measured in liquid money, not total net worth. High net worth that's all locked up in a pension and an apartment gives long-term security, but doesn't help when you need cash this week.

Enter the home: mortgage equity

When you buy a home, the bank doesn't finance the full price — you have to bring part of the money yourself. That part is called "equity," and it almost always has to be liquid. Here's the big catch: your pension doesn't count. Even if you've accumulated hundreds of thousands of shekels in it, you can't pay a home down payment with it. What matters is how much liquid equity you've managed to build.

How much do you need? The Bank of Israel sets ceilings on the loan-to-value ratio (how much of the price you may borrow), and the remainder is your equity:

  • A single (first) home: a mortgage of up to 75% of value — meaning equity of at least 25%.
  • Upgrading home (selling an existing apartment and buying instead): up to 70% financing — equity of at least 30%.
  • An additional or investment property: up to 50% financing only — equity of at least 50%.

On a ₪1.5 million apartment, for example, a single home would require liquid equity of at least about ₪375,000. These rules are updated from time to time, so verify the exact terms with the bank in real time.

Not just the down payment: the extra costs

A common mistake is to think the equity you need is only the down payment. In practice you need extra liquid cash for costs that come with the purchase: purchase tax (which varies by the apartment's value and whether it's your only home), lawyer's fees, broker's fees if any, appraisal and file-opening costs, and finally furniture and moving. Tens of thousands of shekels beyond the down payment is common. So the liquid equity you need to accumulate toward a purchase is larger than the official minimum.

How it all connects

Liquidity is the thread that links your emergency fund to the dream of a home. Both require exactly the same thing — available money, not locked money. The more liquid net worth you build, the better protected you are from surprises and the closer you are to a home. You build that liquidity through the very habits we've written about: consistent saving, an emergency fund, and investing in assets you can reach within a reasonable time.

When you look at your net worth, it's worth asking not only "how much do I have?" but also "how much of it is actually available to me?" The two questions together give the full picture.
