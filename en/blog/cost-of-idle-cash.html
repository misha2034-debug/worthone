Doing Nothing Is Also a Decision: What Idle Cash Really Costs You

7 min read · By the WorthOne team

There's a pleasant, safe feeling in a healthy sum sitting in your checking account. The money is there, accessible, not "risking" anything anywhere. But the uncomfortable truth is that money sitting in cash actually is at risk — it loses value quietly, every year, without you seeing it on a statement. The decision to "do nothing" with your money isn't neutral; it's a choice, and often a very expensive one. Let's understand why.

The silent enemy: inflation

Inflation is the gradual rise in prices over time, which means each shekel buys a little less in the future than it buys today. If inflation runs at about 3% a year, a sum that doesn't grow loses about 3% of its purchasing power annually. That doesn't sound dramatic — until you look at the multi-year picture: at 3% inflation, the purchasing power of money is cut by about 25% within ten years, and by nearly half within 20 years. ₪100,000 sitting in checking for two decades might buy, in the future, what ₪55,000 buys today. The number on the screen is unchanged; what it's worth is not.

That's why "safe" cash is an illusion of safety: you don't see a loss, but you experience one. The loss is simply hidden, because it doesn't show up as a red number — it shows up in the fact that the same amount buys less.

Opportunity cost: what the money could have been

Beyond inflation's erosion, there's a second, even larger price: what the money could have earned had it been invested. That's "opportunity cost" — the difference between sitting in cash and participating in the economy's growth. To illustrate (assuming an average annual return of 7%, for illustration only and not a promise): ₪100,000 invested and compounding over time versus the same ₪100,000 staying in cash — the gap between them after 20–30 years is measured in hundreds of thousands of shekels. And this is the most painful part: there's no tangible loss to point at, just an enormous gain that simply didn't happen. The money didn't go down — it just didn't go up.

"But what if the market crashes?"

That's a legitimate worry, and the answer separates two kinds of money. Money you'll need in the short term (up to two or three years) really doesn't belong in the market — it should stay liquid and stable. But money you won't touch for ten, twenty, or thirty years? For it, the real risk isn't the volatility along the way — it's the inflation that erodes it with certainty if it stays in cash. Historically, broad, diversified markets have recovered from every crisis and continued to grow over time (though that's no promise for the future). For someone with a long time horizon, sitting on the sidelines is the risk — not participating.

How much liquidity do you actually need?

The conclusion isn't "invest everything" — it's to hold the right amount in cash, and no more. A simple framework:

  • Emergency fund: three to six months of expenses in liquid, stable money (a deposit or money market fund). This is the safety cushion, and its place is not the stock market.
  • Planned near-term expenses: money for a wedding, car or home down payment in the next couple of years — also stays liquid.
  • Everything else: "surplus" sums just sitting in checking with no short-term purpose — these are the natural candidates for investing, so they stop eroding and start working.

Many people discover, when they stop to check, that they hold several times more cash than they actually need for security — and that this surplus has been quietly "sinking" for years.

The first step: just see the picture

The problem with money sitting in checking is that it's invisible — it doesn't alert you, doesn't ask for attention, and is easy to forget. The first step is simply to bring all your balances into one place and ask: how much of this really needs to be liquid, and how much is just sitting and losing value? The very question already moves people to act.

The bottom line

Non-decision is a decision. Money sitting in cash beyond your security and short-term needs isn't "waiting safely" — it's eroded by inflation and misses the economy's growth. You don't have to make dramatic moves; it's enough to separate the money that should be liquid from the surplus that can work — and let it start.
