How to Read Your Annual Pension Report (Without Falling Asleep)

8 min read · By the WorthOne team

Once a year, a document lands in your inbox or personal area that most people never open: the annual report from your pension fund. That's a shame, because behind the gray design and insurance jargon hide five or six numbers that affect your financial future more than almost anything else. In this guide we'll go through the report section by section, explain what each number means, what counts as healthy, and when it's time to pick up the phone.

Why bother? Three reasons

First, money: deposit errors, high management fees or an unsuitable investment track cost tens and hundreds of thousands of shekels over time – and the report is where you catch them. Second, insurance: a pension fund includes coverage for disability and death, and you should know what you and your family are entitled to. And third, control: people who know what they have make better decisions. Twenty minutes a year – that's all it takes.

Number 1: The accumulated balance

This is the big number at the top of the report – the total accumulated in your name so far: your deposits, your employer's deposits, and the returns earned on them. Two important checks: does the number make sense relative to your seniority and salary? And did it grow compared to last year's report (beyond the ongoing deposits)? It's also worth writing this balance down for yourself – it is part of your net worth, even if you won't touch it for decades.

Number 2: The deposits – make sure the money actually arrives

The report details the monthly deposits: the employee's share, the employer's share and the severance component. This is where real mistakes surface – an employer who reported but didn't transfer, missing months, or deposits calculated from a base salary lower than your actual pay. Compare a few months against your pay slips. A mismatch here is not a technicality: a missing deposit today is a double hole at retirement, because both the principal and its future returns disappear. If you find a gap – contact your employer and the fund promptly.

Number 3: Management fees – the most valuable check of all

The report shows the fees actually charged, both as percentages and in shekels: a percentage of ongoing deposits and a percentage of the balance. Compare them to market averages and to the legal ceilings (for pensions: up to 6% of deposits and up to 0.5% of the balance). If you're paying close to the ceiling – you are almost certainly overpaying, and one negotiation call can save a great deal of money over the years. Employees of large organizations: check whether a discounted collective agreement applies to you.

Number 4: The return – in the right perspective

The report presents the return your track achieved over the past year. Read it correctly: a single year's return says very little – a red year in the markets will look bad in every fund, and a green year will look good in all of them. What matters is comparison: how did your track perform against similar tracks at other funds, over three and five years? Public comparison tools from the Ministry of Finance make this easy. Consistently poor relative returns over time – a reason to consider moving.

Number 5: The investment track – does it fit your age?

The report states which track your money is invested in. Since 2016, the default is an age-dependent track that automatically reduces risk as retirement approaches – sensible for most people. But if you ever chose a track manually (or someone chose for you), you might be a 28-year-old sitting in a conservative track meant for a 60-year-old – losing decades of growth potential. Check that the track matches your age and risk tolerance.

Number 6: Insurance coverage and the projected annuity

A pension fund isn't only savings – it includes disability coverage (a monthly allowance if you can't work) and survivors' coverage (an allowance for your spouse and children, should the worst happen). The report details the projected amounts in each scenario, as well as your projected monthly annuity at retirement. Check that the coverage levels make sense for your family situation: young singles with no dependents sometimes pay for survivors' coverage they can temporarily waive; parents of young children – exactly the opposite. Adjusting coverage is a significant decision best made with professional guidance.

Bonus: the pension clearing system and "Har Hakesef"

If you want a wider picture than a single report, two free government tools do the job. The pension clearing system (Maslaka) gathers in one place every pension product registered in your name – pension, provident, study funds and managers' insurance – including balances and fees, saving you the chase after reports from different institutions. "Har Hakesef" is a database for locating lost money: old funds and accounts from previous workplaces you forgot existed. People regularly discover tens of thousands of dormant shekels there. Two fifteen-minute checks, once every few years – sometimes with very pleasant surprises.

Red flags – when to pick up the phone immediately

  • Missing deposit months, or deposits that don't match your pay slip.
  • Management fees close to the legal ceiling.
  • An investment track that doesn't fit your age and wasn't chosen deliberately.
  • Incorrect personal details – insured salary, beneficiaries, family status.
  • More than one active pension fund with duplicate fees ("dormant funds" from previous jobs).

A twenty-minute annual checklist

  1. Open the report (or the clearing system, for all your products at once).
  2. Write down the balance and compare to last year.
  3. Verify the deposits against your pay slips.
  4. Check the fees – and negotiate if needed.
  5. Confirm the track and the coverage fit you.
  6. Update the balance in your net worth tracking.

That's it. Twenty minutes of attention, once a year, and your pension fund goes from a "black box" to an asset you genuinely know and control.
