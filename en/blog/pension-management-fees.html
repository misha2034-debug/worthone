Pension and Provident Fund Management Fees: The Guide That Could Be Worth Hundreds of Thousands

8 min read · By the WorthOne team

Imagine there was one financial decision that takes less than an hour and could be worth tens or even hundreds of thousands of shekels by the time you retire. Would you put it off for another year? Most people do exactly that, year after year. That decision is checking your management fees, and it is one of the highest-paying hours of work you will ever do. In this guide we'll explain exactly what management fees are, why they matter so much, and how to check and improve your situation today.

What are management fees, exactly?

Management fees are what you pay the institution that manages your long-term savings – a pension fund, a provident fund or a study fund – for managing the money, the investments and the administration. It's a perfectly legitimate business model: managing bodies employ investment professionals, systems and infrastructure, and they charge for it. The problem starts when savers don't know how much they are paying, and don't realize that the number is negotiable.

Two kinds of fees – and you need to know both

Pension funds in Israel typically charge two kinds of fees, and they stack on top of each other:

  • Fees on deposits: a percentage taken from every new shekel that enters the fund. If you deposit ₪2,000 a month and pay 3% on deposits, ₪60 disappears before the money even starts working.
  • Fees on the accumulated balance: an annual percentage taken from everything you have already saved. Over time this is the more significant one, because it is charged on an amount that keeps growing year after year.

The practical meaning: early on, when your balance is small, the deposit fee stings more. But as your savings grow, the balance fee becomes the dominant cost – which is why veteran savers often pay the highest price for not paying attention.

Why half a percent is never "just half a percent"

Here is the point most people miss: management fees are charged every single year, on an amount that grows through compounding – so their impact compounds too. Take a simple illustration: a young saver depositing over a 35-year career, with a balance that grows steadily. A difference of just half a percentage point in balance fees – say between 0.6% and 0.1% – can add up to a gap of hundreds of thousands of shekels at retirement. Not because of magic, but because every shekel not paid in fees stayed in the fund and kept earning returns of its own, year after year.

In other words: high management fees are not "another small expense." They are a silent partner taking a cut of your future – and unlike returns, which nobody can guarantee, fees are the one factor in long-term saving that is almost entirely under your control.

What do people actually pay, and what's the legal maximum?

Israeli regulation sets ceilings: a new pension fund may charge up to 6% on deposits and up to 0.5% on the balance. For provident and study funds the ceiling is 1.05% of the balance. But the ceiling is not the market price – actual averages are far lower, and new joiners or anyone who negotiates can reach significantly lower fees. Employees of large workplaces often benefit from collective agreements with heavily discounted fees – it's worth checking whether you're entitled to one without knowing it.

How to check what you pay – in minutes

There are three main ways, from easiest to hardest:

  • Your annual or quarterly report from the managing body – it includes an explicit management-fees section, in both percentages and shekels.
  • The national pension clearing system (Maslaka) – a government system that gathers all your pension products in one place, including the fees on each.
  • The personal area on the fund's website – most institutions now present the numbers accessibly.

When you check, note both numbers – deposits and balance – and write them down. You can't negotiate a number you don't know.

How to negotiate fees (yes, it works)

Management fees are not fate, and the managing bodies compete for your money. A few principles that work:

  • Get competing offers. One phone call to a competitor produces a written quote – your strongest bargaining chip with your current fund.
  • Call the retention department. Say explicitly that you received a better offer and are considering moving. Retention reps are authorized to offer discounts you won't get in a regular call.
  • Use turning points. A new job, a raise, or crossing a balance threshold – all natural opportunities to ask for better terms.
  • Get it in writing. A discount agreed by phone must show up in your next report. Verify that it was actually applied.

But maybe high fees are worth it if returns are better?

A fair question. The honest answer: past returns don't guarantee future returns, and a large body of research worldwide shows how difficult it is for any manager to beat the market consistently over decades. Fees, on the other hand, are completely certain – they will be charged every year, in up markets and down markets alike. That's why the accepted logic is to compare fees first between similar tracks, rather than paying a premium for promises.

A practical checklist

  1. Find your current fees (deposits + balance) in every product: pension, provident, study fund.
  2. Compare them to market averages and competitor quotes.
  3. Call, negotiate, and get the discount in writing.
  4. Repeat every year or two – discounts and terms change.

One hour of work, once a year. It's hard to think of a better hourly wage.
