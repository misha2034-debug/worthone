Why Start Investing at 25 and Not "When There's More Money"

8 min read · By the WorthOne team

"I'll start investing when my salary is higher." "First I'll save a bit, then I'll learn the field." "The market is at a peak right now, I'll wait for a dip." If you've said any of these to yourself – you're in very good company, and you're also losing money without seeing it. In this article we'll show why at a young age, with a starting salary and small amounts, you hold the single biggest advantage that exists in the capital market – and why it erodes with every passing year.

Your biggest asset isn't in your checking account

A 25-year-old investor has something no 45-year-old investor can buy at any price: roughly 40 years of investing until retirement. Compound interest – the phenomenon where your gains generate gains of their own – works exponentially, not linearly. That means the final years of an investment period are the most powerful ones, and whoever starts early simply gets more of those powerful years. A shekel invested at 25 can be worth at retirement twice or more what an identical shekel invested at 35 would be – at exactly the same return, with no added sophistication whatsoever.

The real price of "just one more year"

Take a simple numerical illustration (for demonstration only, assuming an average annual return of 7%): someone depositing ₪1,000 a month from age 25 to 65 will deposit a total of ₪480,000 – but the balance may reach around ₪2.4 million. Someone starting under exactly the same terms at 35 will deposit ₪360,000 and reach only around ₪1.1 million. Ten years of delay "cost" more than a million shekels – several times the deposits that were skipped. And it doesn't end with a decade: every single year of delay at the start shaves tens of thousands of shekels off the final result. Time really is money, in the most literal sense.

Second advantage: you can afford volatility

Markets go up and down – that's their nature. But a young investor has a real privilege: their horizon is so long that dips along the way barely matter to them. Anyone investing for 30–40 years will live through several crises – and several recoveries. Historically, broad markets have recovered from every crisis and kept growing, though that is no guarantee for the future. A 55-year-old investor, by contrast, has to be more conservative, because they don't have time to "wait it out." The capacity to absorb volatility is an asset – and you hold it right now, at its peak.

Third advantage: your mistakes are cheap

Almost every investor makes mistakes early on – bad timing in and out, falling in love with a trendy stock, panicking at a dip. Someone who starts at 25 with ₪5,000 will learn those lessons on small amounts, and arrive at their peak earning years an experienced, calm investor. Someone who postpones the start to age 40 will make exactly the same mistakes – but on amounts ten times larger. You'll pay for your financial education eventually; better to pay the discounted price.

"But I don't have enough money for it to be worth it"

This is the most common objection, and it rests on a misunderstanding. The habit matters more than the amount. A standing order of ₪300–500 a month into a broad index-tracking fund does two things at once: it builds a balance whose size will surprise you in a decade, and it builds the behavioral muscle of consistent investing – the one that doesn't panic at headlines and doesn't try to time the market. When your salary grows, the habit will already be there, and you'll only need to raise the amount. The opposite – starting from zero at 40 with large amounts and no experience – is much harder.

How to actually start, without overcomplicating

  1. Make sure the foundation exists: no expensive debt (overdraft, revolving credit) and an emergency fund of a few months of expenses. Debt at double-digit interest beats any reasonable return – clear it first.
  2. Choose a simple framework: for most beginners, a broad index-tracking fund (through a self-managed trading account, a provident fund for investment, or an index track in your study fund) is a common starting point: wide diversification, low costs, zero maintenance.
  3. Set a standing order: a fixed amount, on a fixed date, with no monthly decisions. Automation beats willpower.
  4. Don't peek every day: a quarterly check is more than enough. An investment portfolio is not a news feed.

Wait – what exactly is an "index-tracking fund"?

Since the term keeps coming up, a short explanation is in order: an index (or "passive") fund doesn't try to pick winning stocks. It simply holds all the stocks in a given index – for example, the 500 largest companies in the US – at the same weights as the index itself. The advantages: very broad diversification in a single purchase, especially low fees (there's no team of analysts to pay), and full transparency. For a young investor who wants simple exposure to the growth of the global economy without turning investing into a hobby – it's the accepted starting point worldwide.

What not to do along the way

  • Don't wait for "the dip." Countless investors waited years for a correction that never came, and missed entire rallies. Time in the market beats timing the market.
  • Don't chase the hot thing. A meme stock, a trendy coin or a "tip from a friend" is a gamble, not a plan. The core of a young portfolio should be boring and diversified.
  • Don't invest money you'll need soon. Money for a wedding, a car or a down payment within the next two years does not belong in the stock market.

The bottom line

Nobody can promise you a return. But the mathematics of compounding is clear: an investor's most important decision isn't which stock to pick, but when to start – and the best answer is almost always "as early as possible, even small." Your 65-year-old self won't remember whether you started with ₪300 or ₪500 a month. They'll remember whether you started at 25 or at 40.
