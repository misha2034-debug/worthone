The Study Fund (Keren Hishtalmut): A Complete Guide for Employees and the Self-Employed

8 min read · By the WorthOne team

The study fund – keren hishtalmut – is widely considered the most attractive savings vehicle in Israel, and for good reason. It enjoys a rare tax benefit, it is relatively liquid, and in many cases the employer contributes to it alongside you. And yet many workers don't use it, don't know what's happening inside it, or leave it for years in a track and at fees nobody ever reviewed. This guide puts things in order: what a study fund is, why it's so worthwhile, and how the rules differ between employees and the self-employed.

What is a study fund, in two minutes

A study fund is a medium-term savings product. Historically it was meant to finance professional training – hence the name – but in practice it serves today as a general savings and investment vehicle. The money is invested in the capital market in a track chosen by the saver, accrues returns over time, and can be withdrawn under certain conditions. Its great uniqueness is a combination that barely exists in any other product: a meaningful tax benefit together with liquidity after only six years.

The tax benefit: the reason everyone talks about it

In most investment channels, when you sell at a profit you pay 25% capital gains tax on the real gain. In a study fund, by contrast, a lawful withdrawal of money deposited up to the recognized annual ceiling is completely exempt from capital gains tax. Think about it: if the fund accumulated ₪100,000 of profit over the years, that exemption is worth ₪25,000 that stays with you instead of going to the tax authority. The larger the balance and the longer the horizon, the more the benefit is worth.

Important to know: the benefit has annual deposit ceilings, and they differ between employees and the self-employed. Deposits above the ceiling are possible, but the profit on them is taxable. Check the updated ceilings each year.

Employees: don't turn down free money

For employees, a study fund is usually opened through the workplace. The common structure: the employee deposits 2.5% of salary, and the employer adds 7.5% – a one-to-three ratio. In plain words: for every shekel you put in, your employer puts in three. Even without any tax benefit this would be an excellent deal; with the capital-gains exemption, it's one of the best arrangements available. Keep in mind that a study fund is usually a benefit set in your employment agreement – if you don't have one, it's well worth raising in your next salary negotiation.

Self-employed: the benefit works differently, and it's still worth it

The self-employed have no employer contributing for them, but they get a benefit of their own: deposits to a study fund up to an annual ceiling are recognized as a business expense, reducing taxable income. In addition, their gains are also exempt from capital gains tax up to the recognized deposit ceiling. For a self-employed person in a high tax bracket, the combination of an expense deduction and a tax-free gain makes the study fund one of the most efficient wealth-building tools available – worth considering maxing out each year if cash flow allows.

The six-year rule: how the liquidity works

The money becomes available for tax-free withdrawal six years after the first deposit (or three years in certain cases, such as reaching retirement age). A point many people miss: seniority counts from the first deposit into the fund, not separately for each deposit. That means new deposits made in year five become liquid together with the whole fund at the end of year six. So there's real logic in opening a fund as early as possible, even with a small deposit – just to "start the clock."

Another key principle: the fact that the money is liquid doesn't mean you must withdraw it. A mature fund can keep accruing tax-exempt returns for many more years. Automatically cashing out at year six, with no real need for the money, gives up one of the product's biggest advantages.

Investment tracks and fees: the two decisions in your hands

As with a pension, you choose an investment track for your study fund – general, equity, bond-based, index-tracking and more. As a common rule of thumb, the longer your horizon, the more equity exposure you can consider, according to your personal risk tolerance. And fees? The legal ceiling is 1.05% of the balance, but the market is competitive and significantly lower fees are achievable – especially in index-tracking tracks. Transferring a study fund between managing bodies does not hurt your seniority and does not count as a withdrawal, so there is no reason to stay on bad terms.

Don't leave old funds behind

Changed jobs a few times? You may well have more than one study fund, some of them "dormant" – no new deposits, but fees charged as usual. These funds can and should be located (through the national pension clearing system or the government's "Har Hakesef" lost-money site), and you can consider consolidating or transferring them to a body with better terms. Before consolidating, check the seniority implications: sometimes it's actually better to keep an old, already-liquid fund separate. Either way – money sitting in a forgotten fund with high fees is money quietly wasting away.

Three common mistakes to avoid

  • Not opening one at all. Employees who skip it give up employer contributions; the self-employed lose an annual tax benefit that never comes back retroactively.
  • Withdrawing automatically after six years. With no real need for the money, the withdrawal stops the growth of a tax-exempt snowball.
  • Forgetting about the fund. A track chosen a decade ago and fees never reviewed can cost a lot over time. A short annual check is enough.

The bottom line

A study fund is a rare combination of tax benefit, relative liquidity and investment flexibility. If you're an employee – make sure you have one and that you're using the employer contribution. If you're self-employed – consider filling the recognized ceiling each year. And in any case: know how much you've accumulated, in which track, and at what fees. Three short answers that put you back in control.
