The First Million Is the Hardest: Why the Start Is Slow — and Why It Gets Better

7 min read · By the WorthOne team

Charlie Munger, Warren Buffett's partner, is often quoted with a blunt piece of advice: "The first hundred thousand is a bitch, but you gotta do it. I don't care what you have to do — get there." It may be the most important, and least discussed, insight in wealth-building. Because the real secret isn't which stock to pick or when to enter the market — it's simply surviving the beginning. This article explains why the first stage feels so stuck, and why those who push through it earn the biggest reward.

Why the start feels like nothing is moving

Imagine you deposit ₪2,000 a month. After a year you've accumulated ₪24,000, and returns have maybe added a thousand shekels on top. In other words: almost all the growth comes from your sweat, not from money working for you. That's frustrating — you worked hard and the result looks nearly identical to what you put in. At this stage, investing feels like pushing a stalled car: a lot of effort, little movement.

And that's exactly the trap: most people read this slowness as proof that "it isn't working," and they stop. They don't realize they're on the flat part of a curve that's about to bend sharply upward.

The curve that changes everything

Compound interest isn't a straight line — it's an exponential curve. At first it's almost flat, because returns are calculated on a small sum. But as the balance grows, the annual returns grow with it, and they start generating returns of their own. At some point something magical happens: the annual return on your portfolio exceeds your annual contribution. From that moment, the money earns more than you deposit — the portfolio starts "running on its own."

Reaching that point is exactly what makes the first hundred thousand hard and the fourth million relatively easy. The same dedication it took to accumulate the first ₪100k will accumulate the next ₪100k far faster — because this time the money is helping you.

The math of momentum

Let's illustrate assuming an average annual return of 7% (for illustration only; no return is guaranteed). Say a fixed deposit of ₪2,500 a month:

  • Reaching the first ₪100,000 takes about three years — almost all from deposits.
  • But the seventh or eighth ₪100,000 arrives in less than a year — because now returns do a big share of the work.
  • And the further you go, the shorter the gap between milestones becomes.

The exact numbers vary by amount and return, but the pattern is constant: the first step is the slowest, and every step after it is faster than the last. That's the complete opposite of intuition, which is why so many people fall off along the way.

How to survive the hard stage

If the beginning is the most frustrating part, here's how to get through it without giving up:

  • Make it automatic. A standing order on payday takes the decision out of the equation. You don't "decide" to save each month — it just happens.
  • Focus on your savings rate, not the return. In the first stage, how much you deposit matters far more than the return you'll achieve. That's where you have full control.
  • Measure the trend, not the target. Don't compare yourself to the distant million. Compare this month's number to last month's. A small, consistent rise is all it takes.
  • Remind yourself the slowness is temporary. You're not stuck — you're gathering fuel before takeoff.

Why quitting early is the most expensive mistake

Someone who saves hard for five years, reaches ₪150,000, then gets discouraged and withdraws it all — did exactly the hard part, and gave up right before the reward. They paid the "entry fee" of the slow years, but didn't stay to collect the dividend of the fast ones. Time, not the amount, is the most valuable asset here — and every year you stay invested brings closer the tipping point where money starts working harder than you do.

The bottom line

The first hundred thousand aren't hard because you're doing something wrong. They're hard because that's the nature of the curve — the stage where everything depends on you, before compounding joins the effort. Those who understand this in advance aren't rattled by the slowness; they treat it as an investment in patience. Get through the first step, and everything else is easier.
