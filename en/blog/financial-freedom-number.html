How Much Money Buys Freedom? Calculate Your "Freedom Number"

8 min read · By the WorthOne team

Most of us save without really knowing what for. "For the future," "for a rainy day," "for retirement" — vague goals that are hard to get excited about. But there's another way to think about it: instead of saving "as much as possible," calculate one concrete number that represents freedom — the amount at which work becomes a choice rather than an obligation. This article explains what financial independence really is, and how to calculate your personal "freedom number."

What financial independence really means

Financial independence doesn't mean being rich or quitting work. It means something simple: that your capital generates enough passive income to cover your living expenses, without you having to sell your time. From that point, you work because you want to, not because you have to — and that's a huge shift in the experience of life, even if you choose to keep working exactly as before.

This idea sits at the heart of the FIRE movement (Financial Independence, Retire Early), which has gained popularity worldwide. But you don't need to aim to retire at 40 to benefit from the mindset: simply having a clear target number turns saving from a dreary chore into a journey with a finish line.

The formula: the rule of 25 (or 4%)

Here's the calculation, simply: your freedom number ≈ your annual spending × 25. If you spend ₪12,000 a month, that's ₪144,000 a year, and the freedom number is about ₪3.6 million. The idea behind the multiplier: if you withdraw about 4% of the portfolio in the first year (adjusting for inflation afterward), historical research suggests a high likelihood the money will last for decades, because the portfolio keeps growing in the background. (An important caveat: this is a rule of thumb based on US market data, not a promise — conditions vary by portfolio, period and country.)

Notice the hidden insight here: your freedom number is set by your expenses, not your income. Two people who earn the same can have completely different "freedom numbers" — and someone who lives modestly needs a smaller amount of capital to break free.

Why expenses are the strongest lever

There's a double effect here, which is why it's so powerful. Every shekel you cut from your monthly spending does two things at once: it lowers your target number (because there's less to cover), and it raises the amount left to save (because more cash is free). Cutting ₪1,000 from a fixed monthly expense lowers your freedom number by about ₪300,000, and at the same time channels an extra ₪1,000 a month into investing. No pay raise works in both directions at once the way this does.

Your savings rate sets the timeline

Perhaps the most surprising fact in the whole field: the time to financial independence depends mainly on your savings rate — that is, what percentage of your net income you save and invest — far more than on the salary itself. Someone saving 10% of income will need decades; someone saving 30% dramatically shortens the road; and someone who manages to save 50% reaches independence in less than two decades. The reason: a high savings rate both builds the portfolio faster and trains you to live cheaply — which lowers the target number itself.

How to shorten the road

  • Raise your savings rate gradually. If you're saving 10%, aim for 15%, then 20%. Every extra point brings the finish line closer.
  • Channel raises into savings. If, the moment your salary rises, you keep the same lifestyle, the entire increase becomes "fuel" toward freedom.
  • Invest at low cost. Low fees and a diversified portfolio keep more of the return with you.
  • Check the number every year. Your expenses change, and with them the target. Watching the gap shrink is the strongest motivational fuel there is.

You don't need all of it — partial independence changes lives too

It's important to say: you don't need to reach 100% of the number to benefit from the mindset. Even capital that covers half your expenses ("half freedom") grants enormous freedom — the security to leave a toxic job, take a risk on a new venture, or move to a more fulfilling role even if it pays less. Financial independence is a scale, not a switch. Every step toward it buys you a little more freedom of choice.

The bottom line

Saving without a target is like running without a finish line. Calculate your freedom number — annual spending times 25 — and let it turn everyday saving into a journey with a clear goal. Then remember the secret: your strongest lever isn't how much you earn, but how much you spend and how much you invest of what's left.
